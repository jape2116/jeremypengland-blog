2. Description of context (here, your response should make clear why the focus of, and process for, the thesis is of particular relevance to your context).
The context of this thesis is the Christian Church, and more narrowly focused, clergy members who are neurodivergent. While at first pass this is a broad context, neurodiversity is a relatively popular topic in the disciplines of education and business, as well a trending movement on social media platforms, therefore it is of the utmost importance that theologians and educators inside the Church should be a part of the conversation, not only for the benefit of the laity, but most importantly, in how it supports those who are called to minister to the people of God. 3. Identification of contextual issue in ministry (here, your response should make clear the particular issue, problem, growing edge, or concern that serves as the focus for your thesis project)
The neurodiversity movement is a relatively new way of understanding the uniqueness of an individual and their relation to societal norms. The term was popularized in 1998 by Australian sociologist, Judy Singer[@NeurodivergentWhatIt2022] and refers to the idea that each person’s brain develops in a unique way. Neurodiversity is a term that can encompass everyone (as each person is a unique), however it is often used in the context of referring to an individual who is neurodivergent. Broadly speaking, a person who is neurodivergent is someone who has been diagnosed with a mental disorder as identified by the  “The Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders, Fifth Edition” (DSM-V). Examples of these disorders are ADHD, Autism, Schizophrenia, Depression, OCD, CPTSD, along with many others.

There have been various cultural movements that the Church has had to navigate its way through[^1], and it is now time for the Church to engage with the Neurodiversity movement in a way that incorporates neurodivergent people into the life of the church as worshippers and leaders. There are efforts inside the Church to be more inclusive in its congregations, but those efforts have not yet extended to intentional inclusivity of the clergy. Seminaries have done poorly in educating future leaders in the theological field of disability theology[^2], let alone a relatively new sociological movement of understanding divergent minds. This leaves a large gap in knowledge and understanding of neurodiversity in clergy members and leaders in the Church.	

The best way to begin the conversation regarding the unique circumstances neurodivergent clergy members face is to hear their stories. By doing so, it moves conversations from a theoretical space to ones that impact people.

Identification of one or more theoretical conversation partners (here, your response should make clear what thinkers/theories best help you to visualize your issue/concern and some responses/solutions to that issue/concern; please name specific authors and their books or articles and provide a short rationale for their inclusion):
There are many people who have written in the area of disability theology that have begun to lay the groundwork for neurodiversity inclusion. Authors such as Amos Yong who has written “Disability and the Renewal of Theological Education: Beyond Ableism” and “The Bible, Disability, and the Church: a New Vision of the People of God.” Both of these writings by Yong encourage the Church to look beyond physical and mental limitations to see that people are worthy of being celebrated as creations of God.
	The book “Vulnerable Communion: A Theology of Disability and Hospitality” by Thomas Reynolds offers a vision of radical hospitality that can oftentimes be withheld from people who are different than what is considered “normal.” In particular, he has opened the door for understanding the social disability model instead of the medical model of disability. 
	Erik Carter is an academic who has written about the inclusion of people with disabilities inside the church. In particular “Addressing Accessibility Within the Church: Perspectives of People with Disabilities” and “Including People With Disabilities in Faith Communities: A Guide for Service Providers, Families, & Congregations.” 
	Carter has also worked on a paper with Naomi Annandale that studies seminaries and their approaches to disability theology. It clearly shows a missing element of education that is vital to a large portion of the Church.
	In the area of neurodiversity specifically, there are various authors opening up the conversation for both society and the Church. 
	Lamar Hardwick has written a book called “Disability and the Church: A Vision for Diversity and Inclusion.” This falls into the previous category of authors to engage with, but he also markets himself as the “Autism Pastor” and writes about his experiences.
	Kathleen Nadeau has written “Still Distracted After All These Years: Help and Support for Older Adults With ADHD” and “Career Choices and Workplace Challenges for Individuals With ADHD.” While these aren’t specifically related to the Church, they are helpful works to engage with struggles many people with ADHD specifically may face. 
	Russell Barkley is a leading expert in ADHD and approaches the disorder not as a superpower or totally debilitating, but rather a reality that exists. He has a very balanced approach to the practical realities of living with ADHD that can help provided a helpful framework for leaders in the Church.
	Richard Saul has written a book called “ADHD Does Not Exist: The Truth About Attention Deficit and Hyperactivity Disorder” and though I may not agree with his conclusion, it is important to include this book (and similar works) because it will ensure that the approach to this thesis is well balanced and considerate of the truth above all things.
	Finally, it will be important to reference works such as The Bible, DSM-V, The Americans with Disabilities Act, Rehabilitation Act of 1973, and other governmental documents to know what is expected as it relates to the law and society, and what is expected as it relates to Scripture.
