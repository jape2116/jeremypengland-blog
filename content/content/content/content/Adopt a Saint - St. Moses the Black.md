St. Moses the Black, or the Ethiopian, or the Murin (which means “like an Ethiopian”) [@LifeStMoses2015] was known for his great spiritual transformation from an outlaw to a non-violent spiritual leader who met his death by extending hospitality to the wrong people. 

The beginning of St. Moses' life Egypt started as  a slave to an Egyptian government official. Due to a robbery and alleged murder, he was sent away by his master. With no place to go, St. Moses ended up leading a group of robbers who were known for their ruthlessness up and down the Nile Valley. [@thehumanesocietyoftheunitedstatesSaintMosesBlack2018] These experiences would cause him to live a life of repentance.

When St. Moses discovered the monastic life, he was captured by the discipline of the monks that he repented of his sin and asked to join them in the monastic life. To prove himself, St. Moses committed to a life of pray and fasting in a solitary cell. It was through his unwavering dedication to show his repentance that he was admitted into the abbot.This dedication to overcoming his former life would be a theme in his life.

A large part of St. Moses' life was the struggle in overcoming his anger and guilt of the life had led before becoming a monk. He often found himself speaking with Abba Isidore (the leader of his monastery) who taught him the needed patience and perseverance to seek after God and allow the darkness to be overcome by the light, just as the when the sun rises and gradually takes over night. [@thehumanesocietyoftheunitedstatesSaintMosesBlack2018]

Another lesson we learn from St. Moses is the that outward peace can be used for the benefit of other's seeing the grace of God. At one meeting between St. Moses and Abba Isidore, he asked "what was the value of outward peace if there was no inward peace? The simple reply was that while not perfect, outward calm prevented harm and facilitated God’s grace to others." [@pieterdykhorstStMosesBlack2011, pg.2]

When St. Moses was ordained a deacon, the bishop declared "“Now Abba Moses is entirely white!” The saint replied, “Only outwardly, for God knows that I am still dark within.””[@LifeStMoses2015] When being examined by the bishop, St. Moses displayed great humility in the midst of the testing. Because of his humility in the face of adversity, he was ordained a presbyter.

When he was 75, St. Moses received word that his monastery would be overrun by thieves and resolved to stay behind against the wishes of the other monks. He claimed that "For many years already I have awaited the time when the words which my Master, the Lord Jesus Christ, should fulfilled: "All who take up the sword, shall perish by the sword" (Mt. 26:52)"" [@LifeStMoses2015] Around the year 400 AD, St. Moses and six other monks were killed welcoming the robbers with hospitality into their monastery. [@pieterdykhorstStMosesBlack2011]

St. Moses is a desert father, canonized in the Catholic, Coptic, and Eastern Orthodox churches,[@thehumanesocietyoftheunitedstatesSaintMosesBlack2018] and is the saint of non-violence.




# Personal Reflection
I was initially drawn to St. Moses because of how violent his life was before he entered into the church. The distance between being a murdering robber and a canonized saint is vast. However, it makes the story from the outside much more exciting.

After learning about his upbringing as a cast out slave turned gang leader, I can understand the amount of shame and guilt one must feel when seeking to fully repent and seek after God. It is a struggle that St. Moses dealt with for much of his life, and despite all of that, he was dedicated to God and putting himself into positions that would not allow himself to go back to his old ways. 

Another thing I am drawn to is St. Moses' seeking the advice of others in the midst of his struggles. He finds the monks running from a problem and in order to join them, he dedicates to solitary prayer and fasting until he's proven himself. Then he seeks after the spiritual leader of the monastery he was at and asked him hard questions. Thank God for Abba Isidore and his willingness to teach St. Moses. If it wasn't for his wisdom, guidance, and support, it's possible St. Moses never would have gotten to the point in which he was set free from his demons. 

St. Moses' life reflects the path of holiness in an authentic way. There is the moment in which he turned to God, and then there was the very real, and very long journey to unravel the sin that held onto his heart. I find great hope that through prayer, fasting, and discipleship, I too can follow the steps of St. Moses and face my demons head on.
