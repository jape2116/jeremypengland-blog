Perhaps the most important, overarching theme, between all of the spiritual discipline movements is the desire to be more intentional and less passive about the spiritual life. This is evident when reading about works by St. Francis de Sales, John Wesley, and in this instance, Elaine Heath and Scott Kisker. In many of the movements the aforementioned people are a part of, a complacency has crept into the spiritual life of the communities that each of them call home. There are many similarities between the writings and spiritual understandings between de Sales and Wesley (Heath and Kisker are a continuation of the work of Wesley), more so than the differences one may find between them. 

The first similarity has already been mentioned in the introduction, and that is the impetus for change is dissatisfaction in the spiritual life of the communities surrounding de Sales and Wesley. De Sales was at the tail end of the Protestant Reformation (and the resulting Catholic Reformations) and drew from his experiences of law and spirituality as taught by the Jesuits. This movement was a movement towards the laity's deeper involvement in their own spiritual lives. De Sales understood this well and spoke in a way that many could understand, even those outside of the Roman Catholic Church. De Sales found a way of preaching that connected with the lay people in a way that visual and engaging. This was in contrast to the other priests at his time "who led erudite sermons packed with clever theological references, designed to impress the elite. Instead, Francis became known as someone who preached heart to heart. He conveyed his own passionate love of God and his vision of a world of interconnected human-divine hearts to his eager audiences."[31. @wrightFrancisSalesJane2017] 

John Wesley, and his brother Charles, would also the beginnings of their spiritual movement as a result of the Protestant Reformation. Heath and Kisker write that 
>"As Protestants looked around at their reformed churches they realized their pure doctrine did not necessarily produce the kind of community pointed to in the New Testament. Calls for "further reform" of the church, though not its doctrine, began to be heard, and small communities of lay people, meeting to encourage one another "in love and good works:' began to appear."[47. @heathLongingSpringNew2010] 

In other words, the larger movement of the Protestant Reformation was good, but it wasn't the full answer to a deeper spiritual life. 

John Wesley drew on Anglican and Moravian monastic communities when developing his concepts of bands and classes, and eventually took the concept with him to The Americas in which he invited people into these developing groups to cultivate their spiritual life. These groups were successful because they were consistent (in both time and people) and invited people into vulnerability with themselves, with each other, and most importantly, with God. 

This leads to the next similarity between de Sales and Wesley, and that is community. Wesley's bands and classes relied heavily on people showing up and sharing their hearts with a known group of people. They were not monastic in that they took vows or oaths, nor did they cloister, but they certainly lived in community with one another. De Sales, also believed in community, working to draw the laity into a deeper spiritual life. With the help of Mother Jane de Chantal, de Sales aided in establishing the Order of the Visitation of Holy Mary, which was a monastic order that takes yearly vows instead of lifelong vows of other monastic orders. 

In their book *Longing for Spring: A New Vision for Wesleyan Community,* Heath and Kisker seem to marry the two types of community together. There should be a dedication to one another and to the church (The United Methodist Church in their case). They encourage the taking of vows (yearly) to commit to the way of life that included community, prayer, service, and spiritual growth. "The Order would not be a cloistered community, since it would be apostolic, sent into the world with the good."[55. @heathLongingSpringNew2010] This is in contrast to The Order of the Visitation, which is a cloistered group of nuns, which is also another difference between both Wesley and Heath and Kisker, who are comfortable with mixed gender groups. 

Though the details in the theology may differ, the ultimate goal for de Sales and Wesley was to develop the space for people to grow in their spiritual life. Many times it easy to live in the comfort that is seemingly found in the world, but that will typically lead to complacency. Hopefully, with the renewed emphasis on the communal spiritual life, we will find the growth that de Sales and Wesley both strove for, and ultimately, find no need for great reformations going forward.
