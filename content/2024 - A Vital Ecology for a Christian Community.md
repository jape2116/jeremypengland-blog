# A Vital Ecology for a Christian Community
## Introduction
When a body of people come together surrounding a common interest or identity, it must chose what aspects of the community are more important than others. For example, I was a in a social music fraternity in college. The fraternity, as an organization, has a goal of promoting brotherhood, advancing music in America, and being loyal to a member's alma mater. While that  mission was overarching every member across the fraternity, each individual chapter had a unique identity. Some focused on serving their communities through volunteering, while others focused more on the social aspect of the brotherhood. Some chapters leaned heavily into greek life on campus and others stayed insular to the music colleges. The list can go on and on. The diversity found when meeting members from other schools made the experience of being in that fraternity much more enjoyable because there were unique characteristics that allowed for personality and individuality to shine through in each collegiate chapter while also being bonded together by a common goal and common experiences. 

There is a parallel between a fraternity with hundreds of individual chapters united by a common mission statement and the Church, with thousands of local churches connected through salvation through Jesus Christ. To maintain a unity through Christ while also being uniquely made is a difficult task at times, but when it is done well, it is a reflection of heaven and the multitudes of people who will be worshipping together for eternity.

A healthy and vibrant Christian community requires intentionality and vulnerability from its leaders and members. Leaders are those who are drawing people into a life of vulnerability and members are people who are being drawn further into a life of vulnerability. In this context, the definition of leaders and members/followers may look radically different than what may first come to mind for some readers, which is a superior versus subordinate relationship. While there is importance in having a more formalized leader/follower structure for purposes of administrative and organization purposes, that structure is not as important as being drawn further into following the ways of Jesus.

In Matthew chapter 20, Jesus himself says "“You know that the rulers of the Gentiles lord it over them, and their high officials exercise authority over them. Not so with you. Instead, whoever wants to become great among you must be your servant, and whoever wants to be first must be your slave— just as the Son of Man did not come to be served, but to serve, and to give his life as a ransom for many.”"[@HolyBibleNew2011, Matthew 20:25-27]

In this passage, Jesus himself has not yet laid down his life, so it may have been difficult for the disciples to understand, but his actions required trust and obedience in God the Father and a vulnerability and trust that the disciples would carry on the good news of heaven being near.[@HolyBibleNew2011, Mark 1:15]

At its best, the local church will represent a diverse creation, particularly specific to their context. Further, it will not be scared or fearful of differences when presented with them through new people. At its worst, the Church will be insular, cold, uninviting, and unwilling to see the fullness of creation through diversity. It will use differences to label people as outsiders not worthy of being insiders. Yet diversity should be celebrated and appreciated. 

Jer Swigart and John Huckins write: “When God created, diversity 
was inevitable…We see diversity etched into the created order: 
in plants and animals, the four seasons, and the unique 
personalities and ﬁngerprints of humanity. Creation was and is 
saturated in diversity and God called it ‘good.’ Thus conﬂict 
doesn’t happen because of diversity. There must be something 
more”[@swigartjerMendingDividesCreative2017, 26] To embrace the posture of Swigart and Huckins, those in a Christian community must humble themselves, love the Lord God with all of their heart and love their neighbors as they love themselves.[^1]


## People Dedicated to The Greatest Commandment

### Love The Lord God With All Your Heart
In Matthew 22, Jesus gives "The Greatest Commandment" as a response to very smart religious leaders trying to challenge him on his understanding of the law. The first thing Jesus responds with is "Love the Lord your God with all your heart and with all your soul, and with all your mind."[@HolyBibleNew2011, Matthew 22:37] This command, on the surface looks quite easy, and for those who have proclaimed they are saved through their belief in Jesus, it is foundational to their faith journey. 

The mistake many people will make is that the transformational love of God stops at the grace filled work of the individual, seemingly sufficient to avoid personal and eternal repercussions of not loving God. However, being an intentional and vulnerable Christian community will require a believer to continue on their journey of rooting out sin. This is done through prayer, Bible study and Sunday morning services (all the checklist items of a good American Christian), but it *must* translate into the work of hospitality and restoration of all peoples and creation. 

The journey of loving God with aspects of one's being, begins with fear.[^2] Understanding how powerful God is can be overwhelming, but hopefully one will begin to understand the overwhelming love God has for God's creation, so much so that God would redeem the world through Christ.[^3] This newfound love leads to heart transformation and the beginning of a new faith. But the journey does not end there, it is just near the beginning because "genuine faith is reflected in a genuine change of life, manifested by fruitfulness, service, and good works—not for salvation but because of it."[@maddixUnderstandingFaithFormation2020, 8].This is corroborated by scripture when James writes "But someone will say, “You have faith; I have deeds.” Show me your faith without deeds, and I will show you my faith by my deeds."" and "As the body without the spirit is dead, so faith without deeds is dead."[@HolyBibleNew2011, James 2:18,26]

### Love Yourself
The second part of Jesus' commandment is to "Love your neighbor as yourself."[@HolyBibleNew2011, Matthew 22:39] Though loving yourself comes after loving neighbor, loving oneself must be pursued to fully love a neighbor. In fact, learning to love like Jesus will lead to restoration, and if that can be realized as an individual, it can be realized in the neighbor. "Everyday peacemakers are men and women who commit to ongoing theological renovation and the pursuit of uncommon friendships that shatter the ceiling of our theology, break the shackles of our gospel, and liberate us into cross-shaped, restorative practice."[@swigartjerMendingDividesCreative2017, 149].

Being able to work towards restoration as an individual takes time, effort, sometimes money, and perhaps most importantly, true vulnerability. This is modeled in Psalm 139 when the psalmist writes:

>"Search me, God, and know my heart;
>test me and know my anxious thoughts.
>See if there is any offensive way in me,
>and lead me in the way everlasting."[@HolyBibleNew2011, Psalm 139:23-24]

This simple prayer is an invitation to allow God (whom we are to profess to love with our whole being in the first part of the commandment) to access the deepest and darkest recesses of our hearts. There may be no greater vulnerability that allowing the fullness of ourselves to be on display to someone we love in an act of trust to remove any hurtful attitudes, thoughts, or ways of life. 

### Love Your Neighbor

Throughout the process of learning to love and trust ourselves as we love and trust God, our relationship with our neighbors should be changing to be more full of trust and love. To be able to see oneself as a beloved child of God[@nouwenSpiritualDirectionWisdom2015, 34] should result in seeing neighbors as a beloved child of God. This may be one of the most difficult parts of being a trusting and vulnerable community because it requires the experiences, feelings, history, and reactions of people other than ourselves. 

Loving a neighbor can be as simple as interacting with charity in grace to those people in church with whom there has been a disagreement, or it can be as complex as moving towards dismantling the societal hurts and sins that have been embedded into the laws of the land and the cultural norms of a group of people. 

In order to start developing a community of Christians towards loving their neighbor, those with influence must lead the way in seeking out opportunities to interact with their neighbors. Admittedly, this may seem unnatural and performative at times, but if it is a practice that is done with pray and prompting of The Holy Spirit, then overcoming any feelings of inadequacy is quite possible. It must be pursued for Jesus himself did not shy away from associating with those who had been cast aside by society. "And, further, he is markedly unimpressed by appeals to ethnicity, status, formal religious observance, and gender as means of social worth or worth before God. Thus, divine welcome does not correspond to some type of merit or preexisting social worth, for Jesus' extension of divine hospitality appear as indiscriminate - which is precisely the feature of Jesus' ministry that annoys so many of the religious leaders of his time."[@jippSavedFaithHospitality2017, 18]

So how does one love their neighbor well? Jipp and Huskins offer four practical steps. First, see people as beloved creations of God and understand how we have contributed to the hurt found in the lives of our neighbors.[@swigartjerMendingDividesCreative2017, 71] Second, immerse oneself into the community and learn and be good guests. Recognize that there are no heroes or victims, just people.[@swigartjerMendingDividesCreative2017, 94] Third, move towards the sins with love. Violence begets violence, and while love may difficult to counteract with, it must be done. Do not sit on the sidelines.[@swigartjerMendingDividesCreative2017,131] And fourth, restore people by laying down your own position or authority in favor of relationship and friendship as a way to honor Jesus laying down his life for the salvation of all.[@swigartjerMendingDividesCreative2017, 154]

This process will be ongoing and should start over and over again as there are many parts of lives and in our society in which people have been hurt and their full image of God has been damaged by people who have sinned against them. There are certainly times where it will be frustrating and overwhelming, but it must be done because there is no ending to God's love. What may feel particularly frustrating is that there is no end to the journey and guilt and shame can overtake the identity of beloved child of God. That is why a community of vulnerable and trusting Christians is important in supporting one another. 

In her book titled *Our Unforming: De-Westernizing Spiritual Formation*, Cindy Lee describes the difference of linear thinking and circular thinking. Quickly summarized, linear thinking (common in western cultures) suggests that as we become better Christians, there is less work to be done because we are closer to being holy. Circular thinking (more common in Eastern cultures) allows for a much more winding journey that may bring us back to where started or even more unsettling for some, have no end at all. "Circles remind us that instead of being a destination to reach, the spiritual life is the transformation that is happening within as we experience the sacredness of God, the earth, community, and ourselves."[@leeOurUnformingDeWesternizing2022, 21]

## A Look at Journey Community
### The Mission of Journey Community
The faith community that I am a part of is called Journey Community Church and it is East Dallas. It is part of the Nazarene denomination and in our local context, we strive to follow the ways of Jesus and to be present in our community. Further, we aim to give generously without expectation of return, we invite people into relationship through many intentional events ranging from events in the community like showing movies with free snacks to deeply spiritual practices such as anointing the sick with oil. We strive to be people who recognize that perfection is a myth in need of being replaced by a complete dependance on God. 

### The Practices of Journey Community
One of the core practices of Journey is prayer. The leadership prayers regularly together and individually, there is a prayer team that meets weekly, and on the fourth Sunday of the month, Journey shortens the teaching element of the gathering and enters into an extended time of prayer in which we pray over people, for people, and we anoint anyone who requests. After that gathering time in the morning, we share in a community meal together. It is a beautiful practice that recognizes the reliance and provision of God through prayer as well as the provision and  joy of sharing a meal together. 

As often as we gather, we participate in the Lord's supper, believing that is through the reliance upon God, by the sacrifice of Jesus, and the power of The Holy Spirit, we are able to see the fruit of the spirit manifest[^4] in our own lives so that we are rid of despair, hate, and an unwelcoming attitude to the neighbors we come into contact every day.

The last important practice to highlight at Journey is the openness to seeking the opportunities to be hospitable neighbors. This past year we partnered with World Relief to become a good neighbor team[^5] and have welcomed a family from the Democratic Republic of the Congo into our community. It has been a difficult transition at times, but we felt strongly to seize the opportunity to care for people who were coming from a traumatic experience, while also being shaped ourselves by a family that was from a different culture than our own. It required us to be vulnerable and step out of our comfort zone and into something new and transformative, all the while trusting that the Holy Spirit would guide our steps interactions. 

### Improvements at Journey Community
There always ways to be more vulnerable and trusting in any relationship. Right now at Journey our pastoral staff is quite comfortable with the circular spirituality Cindy Lee speaks about in her book. However, there are leaders in the church who are steeped in their ways and are very used to a linear progression of growth, both of people and the church. Thankfully, they are willing to learn and share the table with the stranger (even if it's an idea). This in it's own way, exposes my own linear thinking. It causes me to slow down and engage with own rush towards movement. 

Similarly, as new people join us in this journey, whether as attendees or community neighbors, we must continue to seek after faithfulness and not "correct" living. Our goal as leaders at Journey is to measure the "effectiveness" of our local church by how the fruits of the spirit are manifesting in the lives of our attendees and the life of our church. This requires a lot of story telling and maturity. It requires pointing out that being faithful is not measured by the amount of people in attendance, but rather by the spiritual life of the believer. Or said another way, "faithfulness is a quality of a mature, consistent, and pervasive faith in the life of the believer, the result of believing the faith over a long period."[@maddixUnderstandingFaithFormation2020, 10] There are no shortcuts to faithfulness. 

## Conclusion

To be a healthy Christian community, there must be trust in God and trust in each other. Trust begets vulnerability, which then leads to radical transformation for love for one another that can only occur by viewing others in the way in which God views creation. This type of Christian community will be honest with one another, they will encourage one another, they will have compassion and grace for one another, and most importantly, they will want to see each person in that community restored to the fullness of their creation in relationship with God. There is no pride or arrogance, anger is rare, and love abounds.
