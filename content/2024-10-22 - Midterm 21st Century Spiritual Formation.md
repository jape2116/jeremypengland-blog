# Ignatian and Salesian Spirituality - Who to Turn to For Your Spiritual Formation
## Introduction

The goal of Christian spiritual formation should be to develop a further love, trust, and dependence on God in every facet of life. While there there are many important figures in Christianity who can point us towards this goal, there are two who have found the ability to share their wisdom beyond time and denominations. St. Francis de Sales and St. Ignatius of Loyola have both articulated practices of spiritual formation that combined the history and depth of the Roman Catholic Church with the lay movement of the Protestant Reformation.

Both de Sales and Ignatius have written in a way that is accessible to the common person, all the while drawing their practitioners into a faith that continually expands to greater depths for the betterment of themselves and their communities. 

This short guide will hopefully serve as a resource in understanding the basics of Ignatian and Salesian spirituality along with some of their best practices in a spiritual journey.

## Overview

**Ignatian Spirituality:** The way of Ignatius is about finding freedom: the freedom to become the person you're meant to be, to love and to accept love, to make good decisions, and to experience the beauty of creation and the mystery of God's love.(Martin, 1)

**Salesian Spirituality:** “a form of spirituality suitable for men and women in every context, not least the everyday world. While appreciative of the contemplative tradition, Salesian spirituality also emphasized service of neighbor, particularly people in need. Francis encouraged spiritual direction for laypeople rather than simply for clergy.”
(Sheldrake, 138)

### Differences:
St. Ignatius came before St. Francis and his writings and Spiritual journey were influenced by the beginnings of the Protestant Reformation taking place in the 1400-1700s. (Sheldrake, 127) Seeking to combine what was good about the Protestant Reformation (lay involvement, holiness for all, movement away from acesitsm) and the teachings of the Roman Catholic Church,  St. Ignatius developed a set of rules that were meant to encourage a deeper spiritual relationship with God and and the ability to discern a  holy life. (Sheldrake, 126) These rules would come to be the foundation of the Society of Jesus, also known as the Jesuits. (Martin, 1)

The most influential work of St. Ignatius is known as "The Spiritual Practices of St. Ignatius." This book guides a participant through a series of four "weeks" of spiritual practices focusing on prayer. And as James Martin writes: "Some classic works of spirituality are meant to be read contemplatively. The Spiritual Exercises are different. They are meant to be experienced, not read. Frankly, they read more like a tedious instruction manual than a moving treatise on prayer." (20) 

This is an important thing to know about the exercises because if you were to sit down and simply read them, you would likely be far from experiencing what St. Ignatius was hoping the participant would experience. The Spiritual Exercises may be better suited to go through with a spiritual guide.

St. Francis, expanding on the contemplative practices from the time of St. Ignatius, would have more of an emphasis on community as it pertains to both the clergy and laity. St. Francis was influenced in part by Jeanne de Chantal, a widow who desired to commit her who life in service to God, but also had the intricacies of motherhood to navigate. St. Francis and Mother de Chantal would go on to form The Visitation of Holy Mary.
>"The group was not meant to be a formal religious order exclusively dedicated to prayer, nor a congregation focused on an apostolic work such as teaching or nursing. Instead, the congregation was intended to be a place for women like Jane: widows, the frail or handicapped, whether youthful or older. It was not a call to intense asceticism but a deep drawing toward and love of God. They were to realize together the transformed vision of a world of intertwined human and divine hearts, the vision that undergirded all Francis de Sales’ writings and pastoral ministry. At the center of this enterprise was the imperative to “Live Jesus!” to exchange one’s heart for the heart of the crucified One who invited all: “Come to me, all you that are weary and are carrying heavy burdens, and I will give you rest. Take my yoke upon you, and learn from me; for I am gentle and humble in heart, and you will find rest for your souls” (Mt 11:28–29). (Wright, 65)

St. Francis' most influential writing is a book called "Introduction to the Devout Life." In his preface, St. Francis writes "Almost all those who have written concerning the devout life have had chiefly in view persons who have altogether quitted the world; or at any rate they have taught a manner of devotion which would lead to such total retirement." (de Sales, 2) Instead of withdrawing from the world, this book attempts to show a person how they can live a devout life to Christ in whatever context they find themselves. 
### Similiarities:
Both Ignatian and Salesian spirituality focus on God being present in all areas of life, therefore everything needs attention as it pertains to a spiritual to the spiritual life. St. Ignatius uses the idea of detachment, which in it's most basic definition, is a posture of living in a way that is not attached to the things of this world, but the things of God. "He once said that if the pope ever ordered the Jesuits to disband, he would need only fifteen minutes in prayer to compose himself and be on his way." (Martin, 18) 

St. Francis uses the idea of devotion to describe the spiritual life of a believer. Early on in his book, St. Francis describes how many people can look devout, but are merely a false representation of a life following Christ. It is much more than rules and appearances, it is the entirety of a person. In his chapter titled "The Second Purification, from all Sinful Affections," St. Francis writes: 
>Be sure, my daughter, that if you seek to lead a devout life, you must not merely forsake sin; but you must further cleanse your heart from all affections pertaining to sin; for, to say nothing of the danger of a relapse, these wretched affections will perpetually enfeeble your mind, and clog it, so that you will be unable to be diligent, ready and frequent in good works, wherein nevertheless lies the very essence of all true devotion (21)

This is very similar to the detachment phase of a spiritual life that St. Ignatius writes about, and it is also similar to the doctrine of entire sanctification found in holiness denominations such as the Church of the Nazarene. 

Both men were dedicated to guiding people towards a life fully immersed in learning and discerning who God is and who we are in comparison to God. Both men's influences extend well beyond the Roman Catholic Church as well as beyond the time period in which they were originally written. 

Both men demonstrated different ways of living. St. Ignatius did so by having rules for the individual spiritual life, and rules of how Jesuits should interact with each other, all the while allowing for grace and flexibility to do what is best for unique situations. St. Francis demonstrated a holy love and partnership with Mother de Chantal. Together they worked to serve those around them and form a community of devoted people who were unique as it pertains to many other societies in the cloistered church, but much more akin to the experiences of the majority of practicing Christians. 

## Resources to Use

**Ignatian Spirituality:** "Spiritiual Exercises of St. Inagtius" 
* As stated earlier, this is a set of practices to be experienced more so than read. The practices aren't meant to be done many times in one's life, as it should take at least four weeks per iteration. 

**Salesian Spirituality:** "Introduction to the Devout Life"
* This book is much easier to pick up and read slowly or even to be read out of order depending on the topic one is searching to grow in. St. Francis himself writes: "If all these exercises were to be performed every day they would undoubtedly fill up all our time, but it is only necessary to use them according to time and place as they are wanted. What a quantity of laws there are in our civil codes and digests! But they are only called into use from time to time, as circumstances arise, not every day." (de Sales, 224)
* The book is broken down into five sections, with each of those sections containing multiple chapters of guidance. 
	* Part I. Counsels and Practices Suitable for the Soul's Guidance From the First Aspiration After a Devout Life to the Point When It Attains a Confirmed Resolution to Follow the Same.
	* Part II. Containing Sundry Counsels as to Uplifting the Soul to God in Prayer and the Use of the Sacraments.
	* Part III. Containing Counsels Concerning the Practice of Virtue.
	* Part IV. Containing Needful Counsels Concerning Some Ordinary Temptations.
	* Part V. Containing Counsels and Practices for Renewing and Confirming the Soul in Devotion.
