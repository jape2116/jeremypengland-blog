Research writing is unique from other forms of writing as it's goal is to address a known problem from a position of neutrality. It starts with a question or problem that the researcher has identified as worthy of being explored and answered. The answer is sought through a diligent and faithful process to seek the truth no matter the outcome the researcher hypothesizes or hopes to achieve. 

Research is accomplished by clearly identifying the problem, studying the work that has already been completed in the relevant field(s), creating meaningful notes and observations, and finally synthesizing the information that has been gathered into a well written and resourced paper. Theological research is recognizing that the pursuit of truth is the pursuit of God as God is the source of all truth. 

While all research should seek to find the answer to a problem, theological research differs from other research primarily in its "objective". This form of writing specifically works towards an "ideal object or topic" where a writer contributes to an already ongoing conversation. [^1] Perhaps the most difficult aspect of theological research is to seek God's truth without bringing any biases into the research process. This is different than a sermon in which a bias and emotion can be used to teach specific doctrine.

If, after the research process is completed in a thorough manner, and it leads to a conclusion that is not expected, it is still the responsibility of the writer to present the information in order to continue the pursuit of God's truth in all things.

Truth seeking is important. Truth seeking in pursuit of God's truth is a holy purpose in which one should treat as a joyful and worshipful experience.


[^1]: Vyhmeister, Nancy J., and Terry Dwain Robertson. _Your Guide to Writing Quality Research Papers for Students of Religion and Theology_. Fourth edition. (Grand Rapids, Michigan: Zondervan Academic, 2020), 43.
